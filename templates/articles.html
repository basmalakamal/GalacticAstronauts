Grown-ups section

Aqua, Latin for water, is a NASA Earth Science satellite project named for the vast volume of knowledge that the agency is obtaining about the Earth's water cycle, including evaporation from the oceans, water vapour in the atmosphere, rain, precipitation, surface moisture, sea ice, ground ice, and snow cover on the ground and ice. Additional variables now being measured by Aqua include radiative energy fluxes, aerosols, plant cover on the ground, phytoplankton and dissolved organic matter in in the oceans, and air, land, and water temperatures.

Launched on 4th May 2002, Aqua has six Earth tracking platforms on board, which gather a range of global data sets. Aqua was initially established for six years, but this initial target is now greatly surpassed. It continues to relay high-quality data from the AIRS, AMSU, CERES and MODIS instruments as well as reduced quality data from a fifth instrument, AMSR-E. Approximately nine months, but in February 2003 the sixth Aqua instrument, the HSB, collected high-quality data

Carbon Monoxide in the Mid-Troposphere over Indonesia Fires, October 26-28, 2015
Widespread forest fires across Indonesia in 2015 destroyed tens of thousands of acres over three months, forcing the Indonesian government to experience high levels of deforestation, loss of life, and billions of dollars. While most fires were meant to clear vegetation during the dry year, a long-term drought worsened by heavy El Niño conditions led to the uncontrolled fires due to lack of rain.
The image displays the global concentration of carbon monoxide at the level of 500 hPa or at about 18000 feet (5 500 meters) altitude, using data obtained by AIRS, the atmospheric infrared sounder in NASA's Aqua Satellite.


Silty Alaskan Skies

A silty powder with grains finer than sand is formed when glaciers scrape against the underlying bedrock. Geologists call it "glacial flour" or "rock flour." This material rich in iron and feldspar also makes its way into rivers and streams, turning the dark, grey, or aqua water. On riverbanks and deltas, when water levels are low, the flour can dry out and become dust for winds to carry into the air. That's what happened on October 28, 2014, when this shot of northern winds blowing dust from the Copper River Delta over the Gulf of Alaska was taken by the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer ( MODIS) on the NASA Aqua satellite. Dust incidents are most frequent in this region in the fall, when river and lake levels are at their lowest in south-central Alaska. In late winter and early spring, another phase of heavy dust activity occurs.


Dust over the Red Sea

This natural-color image of a dust plume flowing over the Red Sea was taken by the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer ( MODIS) on NASA's Aqua satellite on September 1, 2012. The dust mixed over Sudan with the top of the ground below, discernible only by its blurry shape (upper left corner). Immediately off the Sudan coast, the dust was dense, but thinned slightly to the southeast (seen in the high-resolution image).
The Sahara Desert and the Arabian Peninsula rank as the most prolific dust-producing areas in the world on either side of the Red Sea. The dust in this picture originated in northeastern Africa, where fine sediments that can be quickly lofted into the air have been formed by a network of impermanent rivers.
